# Sales Angles — Using the Landscape to Win Deals

> Market knowledge is only useful if it changes how you sell. This is how to apply everything in this repo to actual conversations.

## Lead with insight, not product

The Challenger principle: teach the buyer something about their situation before you talk about your solution. The cloud landscape gives you material to do this in every conversation.

**Opening reframe — use the multi-cloud stat:**
> "Most enterprises at your scale are running two or more cloud providers — often not by design. When that accumulates organically, the governance and cost visibility gap tends to grow faster than the teams managing it. Where are you in that journey?"

This opens a conversation about their infrastructure complexity without mentioning your product. Their answer tells you where the pain is.

**CFO reframe — use the cloud waste stat:**
> "Enterprise cloud overspend averages 30–35% annually — most of it invisible until someone runs the analysis. Has your finance team done a FinOps audit against your current cloud bill? We've seen that conversation unlock budget that was assumed to be already spent."

You're helping them see a problem they don't know they have. That's the Challenger approach.

**Competitive urgency — use the market share shift:**
> "Something that's changed in the last two years is how aggressively Azure and Google Cloud are moving into territory that was previously automatic AWS wins. Are you seeing that play out in how your vendors are positioning? It's changing how a lot of enterprises are thinking about their primary cloud strategy."

You're not claiming anything. You're reflecting a market reality and watching how they respond.

## Positioning against each cloud environment

### If the buyer is AWS-heavy

| Angle | What to say |
|---|---|
| Complexity | "AWS's breadth is also its burden — 200+ services, opaque pricing, steep learning curve for new team members. Most AWS-heavy teams are looking for simplification on top of their existing stack, not more complexity. That's where we tend to land." |
| Lock-in concern | "AWS proprietary services create real switching costs — Lambda, DynamoDB, Kinesis. If they're thinking about multi-cloud or exit optionality, that conversation is usually happening at the VP Eng or CTO level. Are they?" |
| Cost visibility | "AWS billing surprises are the most common CFO complaint we hear in enterprise accounts. The bill is complex, attribution is hard, and FinOps tooling is almost always underfunded relative to the actual cloud spend." |

### If the buyer is Azure-heavy

| Angle | What to say |
|---|---|
| Developer friction | "Azure's developer experience lags — it's a consistent piece of feedback we hear from engineering teams. IT often mandates Azure for EA reasons, but the engineers want something different. That tension is real and it slows things down. How does that play out on your team?" |
| EA gap | "Azure EA pricing is compelling for what's in scope. But EA scope rarely covers the full tooling picture — there's almost always a gap between what the EA covers and what engineering actually needs. We tend to live in that gap." |
| Hybrid complexity | "Azure Arc is powerful, but it's operationally demanding to run well. Teams that are managing hybrid environments often find the tooling overhead grows faster than expected. Is that something you're navigating?" |

### If the buyer is GCP-heavy

| Angle | What to say |
|---|---|
| Regional coverage | "GCP has fewer regions than AWS or Azure — it's a genuine constraint for enterprises with data residency requirements. If compliance is a consideration in any of your markets, that's worth mapping before it becomes a blocker." |
| Enterprise support | "GCP's enterprise sales and support motion is newer than AWS or Azure — account coverage and escalation paths can lag, especially outside major metros. Is that something you've run into?" |
| Expansion confidence | "GCP's pricing and developer experience are real strengths, especially for data-heavy teams. The teams we see having the most success with GCP are the ones who've also invested in the governance and observability layer around it." |

## The FinOps conversation — how to run it

FinOps is one of the most productive entry points into a cloud infrastructure conversation because it is relevant to both technical and financial buyers simultaneously.

**Step 1 — Surface the gap**
> "How does your engineering team currently report cloud spend back to finance — by team, by project, or as one consolidated line?"

Most will say "consolidated line." That's your opening.

**Step 2 — Quantify the consequence**
> "When the bill comes in and finance asks which product line generated the spike — how does your team answer that today?"

They either have an answer (good — they're sophisticated) or they don't (better — they have a problem you can solve).

**Step 3 — Connect to SaaS**
> "We also see this showing up in SaaS-driven infrastructure cost — usage-based SaaS growth can add 20–30% to a cloud bill without anyone explicitly deciding to spend more. Is that something you're tracking separately?"

Almost no one is. Being the person who asks this question puts you in a different category of seller.

## Deals I've seen this apply to

In 11 years selling cloud infrastructure and developer tooling:

**The cloud migration entry point.** The best time to enter an account is mid-migration — budget is unlocked, the buying committee is assembled, and tooling decisions are being made in real time. I've closed deals in 6 weeks that would normally take 6 months by catching accounts at this moment.

**The EA renewal trigger.** When a Microsoft or AWS EA comes up for renewal, every tooling decision in the account gets revisited. Being in the conversation 6 months before renewal — not at renewal — is the difference between being on the shortlist and being an afterthought.

**The FinOps audit outcome.** When a CFO commissions a cloud spend audit, it typically surfaces 3–5 tooling gaps. Those gaps need to be filled before the next board cycle. The sellers who are already in the account when the audit lands win those deals without a competitive eval.

**The acquisition integration.** Post-acquisition, two cloud environments need to be rationalized. Every tool is reevaluated. The seller who understands both cloud environments — and can position their product as the consolidation layer — is in a structurally advantaged position.
