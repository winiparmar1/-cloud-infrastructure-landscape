# Cloud × SaaS Convergence — Where the Two Markets Collide

> The same VP Engineering evaluating a cloud migration is also your SaaS buyer. This is not a coincidence — it's a structural overlap that most sellers miss.

---

## The SaaS sprawl problem

The average large enterprise runs **364 SaaS applications**. A decade ago that number was closer to 80.

The consequence:
- Less than half of licensed users engage with any given SaaS tool in a 60-day window (45% engagement rate)
- Tools purchased outside of IT score higher engagement (54%) — employees find more value in what they choose themselves
- Finance has lost visibility into what's actually being used vs what's being paid for
- Security and compliance teams can't audit what they can't see

This is not just a cost problem. It's a governance crisis. And it creates budget — for tools that solve visibility, rationalization, and governance across the SaaS stack.

---

## SaaS growth creates hidden infrastructure cost

Every SaaS product runs on cloud infrastructure. As enterprise SaaS adoption scales, so does the underlying compute, storage, network egress, and data transfer cost — often billed through the cloud provider, not the SaaS vendor.

This means:
- A company adding 50 new SaaS tools is also growing their cloud bill — even if they haven't deployed a single new internal workload
- Usage-based SaaS pricing compounds cloud cost unpredictably — a 3x growth in active users is not a 3x growth in SaaS cost, but it might be a 5x growth in underlying infrastructure cost
- Finance teams rarely model this. The cloud bill surprise in Q3 often traces back to SaaS growth that happened in Q1.

**The seller's angle:** Ask the CFO or VP Engineering not just about their cloud spend, but about their SaaS-driven cloud cost. Most haven't separated the two. Being the person who surfaces this gap is a credibility moment.

---

## Usage-based pricing is reshaping both markets simultaneously

Both cloud infrastructure and SaaS are moving away from flat seat-based licensing toward consumption-based models. This creates shared FinOps challenges that buyers are only beginning to manage:

| Challenge | Cloud infrastructure | SaaS |
|---|---|---|
| Spend unpredictability | Egress spikes, autoscaling surprises | Usage-based seats growing with adoption |
| Attribution | Which team generated which cost? | Which department bought which tool? |
| Governance | Guardrails on who can spin up resources | Controls on shadow IT purchasing |
| Optimization | Reserved instances vs on-demand | License rationalization |

**The convergence:** Enterprises need a unified FinOps approach that spans both cloud infra and SaaS — not two separate tools. The buyer who understands this is usually the CFO or a newly appointed VP of Cloud Economics or FinOps.

---

## How the cloud provider constrains the SaaS shortlist

This is the structural overlap most sellers miss: **the enterprise's cloud provider often determines which SaaS vendors are even in consideration.**

- Enterprises on AWS frequently use AWS Marketplace as their procurement path for SaaS — vendors listed there get preferential treatment in evaluations and simplified procurement
- Azure shops default to Microsoft-native tooling (Teams over Slack, Dynamics over Salesforce where possible) — and prefer ISVs certified for Azure
- GCP organizations often prioritize Google Workspace integrations and GCP Marketplace vendors
- Security-first buyers require SaaS vendors to be compliant with their cloud security posture — CISO sign-off on the cloud architecture often determines which SaaS tools can be approved

**The implication for sellers:**
Know your buyer's primary cloud. If you're selling a SaaS tool into an AWS-heavy enterprise, being listed on AWS Marketplace can be the difference between being in the eval and not. If you're selling into an Azure shop, your Azure integration story matters more than your feature list.

---

## Engineering teams are the same buyer for both

The VP Engineering or Platform lead evaluating a cloud migration is frequently the same person evaluating:
- DevOps and CI/CD tooling
- Testing and QA platforms
- Observability and monitoring
- Security and compliance tooling
- Developer productivity SaaS

Selling into this persona requires the same technical credibility whether the product is infrastructure or SaaS. The questions are identical:
- "Will this break what we have?"
- "How much work is the integration?"
- "What's the rollback plan?"
- "Who on our team needs to be involved to make this work?"

**The Challenger opportunity:** The seller who can hold a credible conversation about the buyer's cloud architecture — not just their own product — earns a different level of trust. Most SaaS AEs can't do this. The ones who can close faster, at higher ACV, with less competitive pressure.

## What this means for the sales motion

| Situation | What it signals | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer is in the middle of a cloud migration | Budget is unlocked, tooling decisions are active, buying committee is assembled | Accelerate — this is the best time to enter the account |
| Buyer just signed a new cloud EA | They have committed cloud spend and are looking to maximize it | Position your product as value-add on top of their EA investment |
| Buyer is rationalizing SaaS spend | Finance is auditing — budget is constrained but ROI conversations are welcome | Lead with TCO and consolidation narrative |
| Buyer is going multi-cloud | Governance gap is real and growing | Position as the layer that makes multi-cloud manageable |
| Buyer had a cloud security incident | CISO is in the room and budget is unlocked | Lead with security posture and compliance coverage |
