# Provider Breakdown — AWS vs Azure vs Google Cloud

> Written for sellers, not engineers. The goal is not to understand what each service does — it's to understand what each provider means for a deal.

---

## AWS — The incumbent. The safe default. The complexity trap.

**Market position:** 28% share, ~$115B FY2025 revenue. Market leader since 2006.

**What they win on:**
- Breadth — 200+ services, largest marketplace, most certified professionals globally
- Ecosystem — ISV integrations, partner network, community depth
- Default choice — "no one got fired for choosing AWS" is still true in most enterprises

**Where they're vulnerable:**
- Pricing is notoriously opaque. Billing surprises are common. CFOs hate it.
- Console UX is overwhelming — engineering onboarding takes longer than competitors
- Proprietary services (Lambda, DynamoDB, Kinesis) create deep lock-in — a concern for risk-aware buyers
- Market share is declining — from 33% in 2021 to 28% in 2025. Competitors are taking ground.

**The buyer profile:**
Organizations that grew up on AWS, or enterprises that have been AWS-primary for 5+ years. Decision typically made by Platform Engineering or CTO — finance is often a secondary voice.

**Sales angle:**
AWS complexity and lock-in are real objections for CFOs and risk-conscious CTOs. FinOps tooling, cost visibility, and multi-cloud governance tools sell well into AWS-heavy environments. The buyer who is worried about their AWS bill is your opening.

---

## Azure — The enterprise incumbent. Wins on relationships, not technology.

**Market position:** 21% share, ~$100B FY2025 revenue. Growing at 25% YoY — accelerating.

**What they win on:**
- Microsoft integration — M365, Teams, Active Directory, Copilot, Power Platform
- Enterprise Agreement bundling — Azure is often "included" in what enterprises already pay Microsoft
- Hybrid cloud — Azure Arc and Azure Stack are the strongest on-premises bridge in the market
- Government and regulated industries — most government certifications of any provider
- OpenAI partnership — primary home for GPT-4o and enterprise AI via Azure AI Studio

**Where they're vulnerable:**
- Developer experience lags AWS and GCP — engineering teams often resist Azure even when IT mandates it
- Complexity in hybrid setups — Azure Arc is powerful but has a steep operational curve
- Market share declined from 24% in 2022 to 20-21% in 2025 — niche players are chipping away

**The buyer profile:**
Finance, healthcare, government, and any enterprise heavily invested in the Microsoft ecosystem. Budget owner tends to be IT leadership or the CIO — not the engineering team. Different conversation than AWS.

**Sales angle:**
Azure wins on EA bundling — but EA scope rarely covers everything. The gap between what the EA covers and what the engineering team actually needs is your opportunity. Also: Azure's developer experience gap creates internal tension between IT (who mandated Azure) and engineering (who wants AWS or GCP). You can sell to that tension.

---

## Google Cloud — The technical favorite. Growing fastest. Still building enterprise muscle.

**Market position:** 14% share, ~$48B FY2025 revenue. Growing at 28% YoY — fastest of the three.

**What they win on:**
- BigQuery — widely considered the best cloud data warehouse for analytics at scale
- Kubernetes — GCP invented it, and their support is deepest
- Global network — private fiber backbone gives GCP latency advantages in certain workloads
- Pricing — most aggressive discounts and credits for new logos in the market
- AI/ML — Vertex AI, Cloud TPU, and Gemini make GCP the choice for AI-native builders

**Where they're vulnerable:**
- Fewer geographic regions than AWS or Azure — data residency is a real objection for global enterprises
- Enterprise sales motion is newer — account coverage and enterprise support historically lag
- Brand perception — buyers still think of Google as a consumer company, not an enterprise vendor

**The buyer profile:**
Data engineering teams, AI-native startups, organizations prioritizing analytics or Kubernetes-native architecture. Technical buyer drives the decision more than finance — faster cycles, but smaller initial deal sizes.

**Sales angle:**
GCP's regional footprint gap is a genuine objection for compliance-sensitive enterprises. If data residency is a concern, this is your opening. On the flip side — GCP's pricing and developer experience make it a strong expansion play inside organizations already experimenting with it.

---

## The "Others" — 37% of the market

The remaining third of the market is fragmented across:

| Provider | Niche |
|---|---|
| Oracle Cloud | Oracle database workloads, ERP-adjacent enterprises |
| IBM Cloud | Legacy enterprise, mainframe-adjacent, regulated industries |
| Alibaba Cloud | APAC, China-market-dependent organizations |
| Regional players (OVHcloud, IONOS, Hetzner) | Data sovereignty, GDPR-strict European enterprises |
| Specialist GPU clouds (CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, Nebius) | AI training and inference workloads |

**Why this matters for sellers:** Enterprises choosing regional or specialist providers often do so for compliance or cost reasons — not technical preference. Understanding why a buyer chose a non-hyperscaler tells you a lot about what they prioritize.
