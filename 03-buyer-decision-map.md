# Buyer Decision Map — How Enterprises Actually Buy Cloud Infrastructure

> The stated reason for a cloud decision is rarely the real reason.

---

## The real decision drivers — ranked by frequency

### 1. Existing vendor relationships and Enterprise Agreements

Most cloud decisions don't start from a blank evaluation. They start from:
- "We already have an Azure EA through our Microsoft agreement"
- "Our engineers all know AWS — retraining would take a year"
- "We signed a GCP commitment to unlock $500K in credits"

Displacing a primary cloud provider is a multi-year motion. Selling alongside the existing cloud is faster and more winnable. Know what cloud your buyer is already on before you position anything.

### 2. Security, compliance, and data residency

Regulated industries lead with compliance — not performance or price. For finance, healthcare, and government buyers, the question is not "which cloud is best" but "which cloud is certified for our regulatory environment."

- HIPAA, SOC 2, ISO 27001 — all three hyperscalers meet these
- FedRAMP — Azure leads significantly in government certifications
- GDPR and data residency — regional players like OVHcloud and Hetzner win on this specifically
- Zero-trust architecture — all three hyperscalers now offer it; differentiation is in implementation

If CISO is in the room early, the technical evaluation has already happened. You're in a compliance conversation.

### 3. Cost visibility and FinOps pressure

Cloud waste is the elephant in the room for every enterprise CFO. Estimates put enterprise cloud overspend at 30–35% annually — most of it invisible until someone runs the analysis.

The FinOps challenge is structural:
- Finance sees the cloud bill but doesn't understand what generated it
- Engineering understands the spend but doesn't control the narrative to finance
- Without chargeback attribution (spend by team, by project, by product line), the CFO can't make informed decisions

This gap is a sales opportunity. Tools that create cost visibility, chargeback models, and budget guardrails solve a problem that is felt at the C-suite level — not just in engineering.

### 4. Multi-cloud risk mitigation

89% of enterprises use multiple cloud providers. The majority of multi-cloud environments were not planned — they accumulated through acquisitions, team preferences, and departmental purchasing. The drivers are:

- Avoiding vendor lock-in (most cited reason per Gartner)
- Best-of-breed service selection across providers
- Disaster recovery and geographic redundancy
- Regulatory requirements for data distribution

Multi-cloud creates governance complexity. Every enterprise running two or more clouds has a tooling and visibility gap. That gap is the budget for cloud management, observability, security, and DevOps SaaS.

### 5. Developer experience and team skill set

Engineering teams resist migrations that break their workflows. The platform that the majority of engineers know tends to win the internal debate — even when it's not the technically optimal choice.

This creates a split buying committee:
- IT leadership wants standardization and control (often pushes Azure or AWS for EA reasons)
- Engineering teams want developer experience and flexibility (often prefer GCP or AWS)
- Finance wants cost predictability (wants whatever is cheapest to govern)

The seller who understands this tension — and positions their product as the layer that resolves it — has a structural advantage.

## The buying committee — personas and what they actually care about

| Persona | Primary concern | What they're afraid of | How to win them |
|---|---|---|---|
| CTO | Strategic fit, vendor consolidation, technical debt reduction | Making a 5-year mistake | One clear narrative: this simplifies, not adds complexity |
| VP Engineering | Developer velocity, platform reliability, team overhead | Their best engineers quitting | Show the reduction in toil — hours saved, incidents avoided |
| Platform / DevOps lead | Stability, rollback, integration with existing stack | Breaking something at 2am | Technical credibility first — they ask hard questions before they trust |
| CFO | Total cost of ownership, chargeback attribution, budget predictability | A cloud bill surprise in Q3 | Translate infrastructure decisions into $ outcomes |
| CISO | Security posture, compliance certification, data residency | A breach that starts with a vendor | Compliance coverage by workload — give them the audit-ready story |
| Procurement | Contract terms, SLAs, exit clauses | Vendor lock-in | Multi-year terms with clear exit rights and portability |

## The decision timeline reality

Enterprise cloud infrastructure decisions move slowly. Expect:

- **Initial evaluation to shortlist:** 2–4 months
- **POC or pilot phase:** 4–12 weeks
- **Commercial negotiation:** 4–8 weeks
- **Total cycle from first meeting to signed contract:** 6–18 months for net-new platform decisions

The deals that move faster are **expansion** deals — adding a new product or workload on top of an existing cloud relationship. If you can land a small initial use case, expansion cycles are 50–70% shorter.

## What actually accelerates a decision

1. A board or executive mandate to reduce cloud spend or improve reliability
2. A security incident that exposes an infrastructure gap
3. A competitor moving to a unified platform (the "if they're doing it" trigger)
4. A CFO-initiated FinOps audit that surfaces previously invisible spend
5. An acquisition or merger that forces infrastructure consolidation

None of these are things you can create. But you can be positioned to capitalize on all of them — which is why account coverage and multithreading matter more in cloud infra sales than almost any other category.
